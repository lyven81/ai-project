An honest account of what this tool does and does not claim, and what it takes to move it from a portfolio demo to a live claims desk. Built to be checked, not taken on faith.

How a claim moves through it

1

Read the claim facts

The AI reads only the claim details: vehicle, accident area, who was at fault, days to report, police report, prior claims. It is never told the real outcome.

2

Judge and explain

It returns a handling lane, a fraud risk from 0 to 1, how confident it is, and a one-line reason in plain words.

3

Route by a fixed rule

A simple rule, not the AI, decides routing: risk at or above the dial goes to investigation, repudiation candidates always go to a person, only clean low-risk claims fast-track.

4

Score against the truth

Only now do we reveal the real fraud outcome and tally where the AI was right and wrong. That is the scorecard.

What is measured and what is not

Honesty matters more than a flattering number, so this is stated plainly:

  • Fraud is scored.  The data carries a real fraud label, so the fraud flags are measured against it. That is the scorecard you see.
  • The other lanes are routing, not graded.  Fast-track, standard, and complex are operational sorting. No public truth label exists for them, so they are shown as a distribution, never claimed as accurate.
  • Fraud is rare.  In real motor books fraud runs near 6 in 100. This demo sample is rebalanced so there are enough fraud cases to measure, which is disclosed rather than hidden.

The rules that never bend

  • Decision support only. A human assessor owns every flag and every repudiation.
  • No auto-denial of claims. A fraud flag is a trigger to review, not an accusation.
  • The model is pinned to a dated version so results can be reproduced.
  • Synthetic and public data, used for portfolio demonstration only.

From this demo to a live claims desk

What changes when the same design runs on a real, growing claims feed.
→

Score once, then re-score weekly

The demo classifies a labelled sample live in the browser. Production runs one offline batch over the full claims feed, then re-scores weekly, so cost and latency stay flat as volume grows.

→

Pin the model, cache the verdict

For results an auditor can reproduce, the model is pinned to a dated snapshot and each verdict is cached with the claim, so the same claim always returns the same lane and risk.

→

Wire the gate to the queue

The deterministic gate becomes a routing rule in the claims system: SIU flags open an investigation task, repudiation candidates go to a senior assessor, fast-track clears with a logged reason.

→

Keep the human in front

Nothing is auto-denied. The model triages and explains; an assessor owns every flag, and the weekly scorecard is how the team keeps trusting, or challenging, the tool.
