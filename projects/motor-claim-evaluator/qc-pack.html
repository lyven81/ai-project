1. Claims Classification Guide

One entry per handling lane: what it means, when to route there, when to escalate, the edge cases, and a worked example.

Fast-track

DefinitionLow value, clear liability, documents complete, no fraud signals. Clears with minimal effort.
Route ifLow price band, police report on file, timely reporting, no prior-claims pattern, fraud risk well below the dial.
Escalate ifAny late reporting, missing police report on a non-minor loss, or a prior-claims pattern appears. Move to Standard or SIU.
Edge casesLow value but third-party fault unclear: keep in Standard until liability is settled. Never fast-track a total loss.
ExamplePerodua, urban, policy holder at fault, low value, police report Yes, reported within days, no prior claims.

Standard review

DefinitionNeeds a human assessment, nothing alarming. The default lane for ordinary claims.
Route ifMid value or some missing context, but no fraud indicators and no high-exposure signal.
Escalate ifHigh exposure surfaces (route to Complex) or fraud indicators appear (route to SIU via the gate).
Edge casesMid value with one soft fraud signal: leave in Standard but note the signal for the assessor.
ExampleToyota, urban, third party at fault, mid value, police report Yes, one prior claim.

Complex / high-value

DefinitionHigh exposure. Route to a senior adjuster regardless of fraud signal.
Route ifHigh price band, total loss, or large potential payout.
Escalate ifFraud indicators also present: the gate pulls it to SIU, but the senior adjuster stays involved on exposure.
Edge casesHigh value with clean documents is still Complex, not Fast-track. Value alone justifies senior eyes.
ExampleMercedes, high value band, total loss, clear liability, all documents present.

Investigate (SIU)

DefinitionFraud indicators present. Refer to the Special Investigations Unit. This is the only lane scored against the real label.
Route ifFraud risk at or above the dial, or the model itself routes to SIU. Common triggers: late report, prior-claims pattern, no police report on a material loss.
Escalate ifStrong evidence of organised fraud: refer beyond SIU to the fraud manager and, where required, law enforcement.
Edge casesAn SIU flag is a review trigger, never an accusation and never an auto-denial. A cleared SIU claim returns to its routing lane.
ExampleReported more than 30 days after the accident, police report No, two to four prior claims, third party at fault.

Repudiate-candidate

DefinitionLikely outside cover (lapsed policy, excluded driver). Flagged for a human, never auto-rejected.
Route ifFacts suggest a coverage problem rather than a fraud problem.
Escalate ifCoverage problem plus fraud indicators: SIU and the coverage reviewer both take it.
Edge casesAlways reaches a person. A repudiation is a legal decision a human owns, with reasons recorded.
ExampleAccident date falls outside the policy period, or the named driver is excluded under the policy terms.

2. Prompt Library

The single classifier prompt that drives the evaluator, plus a tie-breaker for borderline lanes and a re-check for high-stakes flags. All three see claim facts only, never the outcome.

2.1 Primary classifier prompt

You are a motor-insurance claim triage assistant. Use the claim facts only.
Never assume or guess the final fraud outcome; judge from the facts in front of you.
Return a handling lane, a fraud_risk from 0 to 1, your confidence from 0 to 1,
and a short reason (<=20 words).

Lanes:
- Fast-track: low value, clear liability, documents complete, no fraud signals.
- Standard review: needs a human assessment, nothing alarming.
- Complex / high-value: high exposure, route to a senior adjuster.
- Investigate (SIU): fraud indicators present (late report, prior-claims pattern, no police report).
- Repudiate-candidate: likely outside cover (lapsed policy, excluded driver); flagged for a human, never auto-rejected.

Output strict JSON: { "lane", "fraud_risk", "confidence", "reason" }

2.2 Tie-breaker prompt (borderline lane)

Used when two lanes are close, for example Standard versus SIU, or Complex versus Fast-track.

The claim below was classified with low confidence between two lanes.
Decide which single lane fits best using the claim facts only.
Weigh fraud indicators (late report, prior claims, missing police report) for SIU,
and exposure (value, total loss) for Complex. When genuinely unsure, prefer the
more cautious lane: SIU over Standard, Standard over Fast-track.
Return the same strict JSON: { "lane", "fraud_risk", "confidence", "reason" }

2.3 Re-check prompt (high-stakes flag)

A second pass before an SIU or Repudiate-candidate flag reaches a person, to reduce false alarms.

This claim was flagged as Investigate (SIU) or Repudiate-candidate.
Re-read the facts and state, in one line, the single strongest fact supporting
the flag and the single strongest fact against it. Then confirm or downgrade the
lane. Do not auto-deny; a flag is a review trigger, not a decision.
Return: { "lane", "fraud_risk", "confidence", "reason", "for", "against" }

4. QC Standard Operating Procedure

How an adjuster team runs the review queue day to day, and the weekly scorecard that keeps the tool honest.

4.1 Daily review queue

  1. Open the queue by lane. Work SIU and Repudiate-candidate first, then Complex, then Standard. Fast-track is cleared in a batch with a logged reason.
  2. Read the model's reason. Every claim carries a one-line reason and a fraud risk. Confirm it against the facts before acting.
  3. Apply the re-check prompt to any SIU or Repudiate-candidate flag before it leaves the desk, to cut false alarms.
  4. Decide and record. Confirm, downgrade, or escalate. A fraud flag is a review trigger, never an auto-denial; a repudiation is a human legal decision with reasons recorded.
  5. Log the outcome. Note the final lane and, once known, the true fraud outcome. This feeds the weekly scorecard.

4.2 Weekly QC scorecard

  1. Pull the week's closed claims that now carry a confirmed fraud outcome.
  2. Recompute the scorecard: catch rate (recall), flag accuracy (precision), false-alarm rate, F1, and accuracy at high confidence, each with a 95% confidence interval.
  3. Check calibration. When the model said high risk, did those claims actually turn out fraudulent more often? If not, the threshold needs review.
  4. Set the dial for next week. Move the fraud-risk threshold to the agreed point on the catch-rate versus false-alarm trade-off, and record why.
  5. Review drift. If catch rate falls or false alarms rise two weeks running, re-score a fresh sample and consider re-pinning the model snapshot.
The scorecard is the trust contract. The team keeps using the tool only as long as the weekly numbers hold up, and challenges it the moment they do not.
