# Food Culture

## The Breakfast Ritual
Sarawak Laksa is a morning dish. Eating it for lunch is met with mild puzzlement. The ritual: wake up, drive to your stall, order laksa and Teh C Peng, sit at a plastic table, and eat before the day begins.

## The Kopitiam Setting
Inseparable from the kopitiam format — open-air, marble-topped tables, overhead fans, mixed community seating. Hawker stalls rent space; the kopitiam supplies drinks. No dress code, no reservation, no menu.

## Multi-Community Ownership
Cooked by Chinese, Malay, and Iban families alike. The recipe has passed through communities over generations. Halal and non-halal versions exist side by side with little controversy. The dish belongs to everyone.

## The Diaspora Longing
Ask any Sarawakian living in KL, Singapore, or overseas what they miss most — laksa comes up within the first three answers. Cap Helang paste ships internationally. Diaspora communities keep their home-cooked laksa as connection to Sarawak.

## The Paste as Heritage
Many families keep their own laksa paste recipe, passed down from grandmothers. Rarely written down — it lives in memory, learned by watching, adjusted by smell. Commercial pastes exist because not everyone can make it from scratch, but the homemade version remains the gold standard.

## Stall Loyalty
Kuching residents return to the same stall for years or decades. Recommending a stall is hospitality — locals take visitors to their stall, not the most famous one.
